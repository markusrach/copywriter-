The Coca-Cola Lesson

In 2024 and 2025, Coca-Cola used generative AI to remake its iconic "Holidays Are Coming" ad. Both attempts faced public backlash — viewers called the results "soulless" and "creepy." Five AI specialists spent a month generating 70,000 video clips. The result? Audiences rejected it.

The lesson isn't that AI is bad at copy. It's that great copy needs emotional truth, specificity, and human stakes. AI can mimic structure but struggles with Laws 2 (momentum), 4 (specificity), and 7 (selling, not just describing). The brands that failed with AI in 2025 — McDonald's, H&M, Vogue — all made the same mistake: they replaced the human voice instead of augmenting it.

The Takeaway for Your Career

AI will write first drafts faster than you ever could. That's not your competition. Your competitive advantage is the ability to diagnose why copy fails, apply frameworks like the 7 Laws to fix it, and inject the emotional specificity that makes someone stop scrolling. The copywriter's job isn't disappearing — it's evolving from writer to editor, strategist, and quality controller.

Part III — Beyond Human Readers

The Agent Economy

You just learned to write for humans. But what happens when the buyer isn't reading at all?

Beat 1 — The Split

Not All Purchases Are Equal

AI agents are already handling routine purchases — reordering supplies, comparing insurance, selecting the cheapest compatible printer ink. But they're not booking honeymoons or choosing birthday gifts. The divide is structural: functional purchases are being automated first. Hedonic purchases remain human.

H6 (Rach, 2026): "The substitution effect and choice liquidity are stronger for utilitarian products and high-risk purchase contexts than for hedonic products and low-risk contexts."

The "contested" zone is where the next decade of marketing strategy will be fought. Wine can be selected by specs (region, rating, price) or by story (the vineyard you visited on holiday). Groceries can be auto-replenished — until someone wants to cook something new. The boundary is fluid, and it moves as agent capabilities improve.

Beat 2 — The Inversion

Attention Is Dead. Long Live Exposure.

Every law you learned in Part I is built on one assumption: human attention is scarce. You have 1.5 seconds. Headlines carry 80% of the weight. Every sentence must earn the next. But agents don't skim. They read everything. They don't get bored. They don't scroll past. For a machine, exposure equals attention.

This inverts the entire logic of advertising. Your dual-pathway framework shows why:

Beat 4 — Choice Liquidity

Your Consideration Set Is Being Restructured

Choice liquidity is the algorithmically driven reconfiguration of which brands you even consider — across repeated purchase episodes. It's not switching. It's the structural condition that makes switching possible. And it's bidirectional.

Choice Liquidity Meter
Trust calibration
UNCONDITIONAL TRUST CALIBRATED TRUST
H4: "Repeated reliance on AI recommendations increases choice liquidity — manifesting as both expansion and echo-chamber narrowing depending on trust calibration." — H7: "Transparency, explainability, and user control moderate trust transfer; calibrated trust reduces echo-chamber narrowing."

For high-equity brands, this is amplification — AI trust reinforces existing brand equity (H2). For low-equity brands, it's substitution — AI trust compensates for weak brand associations. The strategic implication: if you're an incumbent, agents may protect you. If you're a challenger, agents may be your best distribution channel — provided your data is machine-readable.

The Open Questions

Three Provocations for Discussion

1. If agent-mediated purchase decisions follow the AI-mediated route rather than the brand-equity route, what is the residual function of brand equity in utilitarian categories?
2. Does choice liquidity favor incumbents (amplification for high-equity brands) or challengers (substitution for low-equity brands) — and does that answer change when the buyer is a machine?
3. If attention is no longer scarce for the buyer, is the entire edifice of advertising — built on the assumption of attention scarcity — structurally obsolete for functional categories?
